The privacy promise

Your audio stays where it belongs.

Your audio never leaves your device; no uploads, no accounts, no telemetry; all processing local.

That claim rests first on what you can audit: Audio Cutter declares zero permissions, and CI scans both browser builds for network primitives such as fetch, XMLHttpRequest, WebSocket, sendBeacon, and EventSource. A strict no-egress content-security policy (connect-src 'none') is defense in depth, not the sole proof: a CSP does not restrict top-level navigation, so source review also verifies that the extension opens only its own bundled app page. CI checks the empty permission list and CSP in both built manifests. Read the peer review and technical notes.

How privacy is enforced

The receipt, not just the promise.

Audio Cutter ships with an empty permission list and a strict no-egress content-security policy. CI checks both against the built Chrome and Firefox manifests on every change.

Declared permissions

"permissions": []

Extension-page content-security policy

default-src 'none'; script-src 'self'; style-src 'self' 'unsafe-inline'; img-src 'self' data: blob:; media-src 'self' blob:; worker-src 'self'; connect-src 'none'; form-action 'none'; frame-src 'none'; object-src 'self'; base-uri 'none'

connect-src 'none' blocks outbound connections from extension pages, while form-action 'none' and frame-src 'none' close other egress paths. Saving audio uses a standard browser download from an in-page blob, so no downloads permission is requested. Empty permissions and CSP are not proof by themselves; the claim is backed by source review, built-bundle scans, manifest checks, and production-artifact tests. See the capability contract.
